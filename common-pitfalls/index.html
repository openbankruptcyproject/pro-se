Most Common Pro Se Procedural Failures

Across thousands of pro se bankruptcy filings, several procedural failures recur. This page catalogs them so pro se filers know what to watch for — and so court-based pro se assistance programs can prioritize coaching.

Top 10 pro se procedural failures

1. Missing the credit-counseling certificate (pre-petition)

11 U.S.C. § 109(h) requires a credit-counseling course within 180 days before filing. The certificate must be filed with the petition. Pro se filers commonly file without taking the course, or take the course but forget to file the certificate. Result: case dismissed without prejudice, but filing fee and time wasted.

2. Missing the financial-management course (pre-discharge)

11 U.S.C. § 727(a)(11) requires the debtor to complete a personal-financial-management course before discharge. Pro se filers often forget or assume completion occurs automatically. Result: discharge denied or delayed.

3. Inaccurate or incomplete schedules

Schedules A-J require careful inventory of assets, debts, income, and expenses. Common errors:

  • Failing to list assets (vehicles, retirement accounts, anticipated tax refunds)
  • Listing debts at the wrong amount or in the wrong category
  • Missing creditors entirely (which limits discharge protection for those debts)
  • Income reporting that doesn't match Schedule J expenses or means-test calculations

4. Statement of Financial Affairs (SOFA) failures

SOFA Question 5 (events preceding bankruptcy) and Question 18 (transfers within prior years) commonly trip pro se filers. Inaccurate or omitted answers can lead to discharge objections or trustee investigations.

5. Incorrect exemption claims

Choosing the wrong exemption scheme (federal vs state) or claiming exemptions in excess of allowable caps. Some pro se filers use the federal exemption table even in opt-out states, which doesn't work.

6. Missing the § 341 meeting

Pro se filers occasionally miss the meeting of creditors entirely, particularly when the notice goes to a stale address or the filer doesn't realize attendance is mandatory. Result: dismissal.

7. Means-test errors

Above-median pro se filers must complete Form 122A-2 (Chapter 7) or 122C-2 (Chapter 13) means-test calculations. Common errors include using outdated IRS expense standards, incorrect priority-debt deductions, and arithmetic mistakes.

8. Failing to address secured debt properly

Reaffirmation agreements (for keeping secured debt post-discharge) require a Form 240A signed by both debtor and creditor, with a hearing if pro se. Pro se filers often fail to file timely reaffirmation, losing the option to keep the secured collateral.

9. Chapter 13 plan-payment defaults

For Chapter 13, missing 1-2 plan payments without seeking a plan modification typically triggers a trustee motion to dismiss. Pro se filers often don't realize they can file a modification motion or how to do so, and the case gets dismissed after a payment default.

10. The § 1328(f) trap

Filing Chapter 13 within the lookback period after a prior bankruptcy discharge produces no Ch.13 discharge (per § 1328(f)). Pro se filers commonly file successive Ch.13 cases not knowing about the bar, complete the plan, and learn at discharge stage that no discharge will issue. See the § 1328(f) research project.
