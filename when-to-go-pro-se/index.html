When Self-Representation May Be Reasonable

For most bankruptcy filings, attorney representation produces better outcomes. But there's a narrower set of cases where pro se filing is a reasonable choice. This page identifies those patterns.

The strongest pro se candidates

Cases where pro se filing has the best chance of success:

  • Chapter 7, below-median income, no real property. The "no-asset" Chapter 7 case is the simplest pattern. Trustees know what to expect; the case proceeds on a relatively rote timeline.
  • All consumer debt, no business debts. Business-related debt complicates schedules and means-test analysis.
  • No secured debt, or only one car loan with current payments. Secured debt requires reaffirmation analysis and potentially redemption considerations.
  • No prior bankruptcy filings. Successive filings trigger § 362(c) stay rules and § 1328(f) discharge bars that complicate pro se cases.
  • Local court has a strong pro se help desk. Court-based assistance materially improves pro se outcomes.

When pro se is risky despite simplicity

Even simple-looking cases can be derailed by:

  • Income spikes in the 6 months pre-petition (means-test arithmetic gets harder)
  • Recent transfers to family or friends (avoidance-power risk)
  • Anticipated tax refunds (must be properly disclosed and exempted)
  • Lawsuits already pending (may involve complex stay considerations)
  • Pre-petition payment of any single non-priority creditor over a threshold (preference exposure)

When pro se is generally inadvisable

Patterns where attorney representation is strongly preferred:

  • Chapter 13 case (plan drafting and confirmation are highly technical)
  • Chapter 11 / Subchapter V (essentially require counsel)
  • Real estate involved (secured-debt and exemption strategy is complex)
  • Above-median income (means-test calculation is technical)
  • Anticipated objections (creditor disputes, fraud allegations, complicated debt mix)
  • Spouse with separate filing or contested ownership of property
  • Pending divorce or family-law matters
  • Substantial business interests (LLC member, partnership, etc.)

Hybrid approach: unbundled legal services

Some attorneys offer "unbundled" or "limited-scope" representation:

  • Pay for an initial consultation and case-strategy review
  • Pay for specific document drafts (schedules, plan, motions)
  • Self-represent for routine filings and the § 341 meeting
  • Bring counsel back for any contested matters

This can dramatically lower the total cost while preserving professional input on the high-value decision points.

Final caution

The "pro se" decision is fact-specific. The most reliable filter is: can you find a free or low-cost consultation with a bankruptcy attorney? Many offer free initial consultations. Even if you ultimately file pro se, the consultation will help you identify whether your case is in the simple zone or the complex zone.
