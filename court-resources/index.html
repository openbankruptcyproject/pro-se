Court-Based Pro Se Resources

Most federal bankruptcy courts have pro se assistance programs. Quality and scope vary widely. This page surveys the typical resources and where pro se filers can find help.

Court-based help desks

Many bankruptcy courts have walk-in help desks staffed by volunteer attorneys, court clerks, or third-party legal-aid organizations:

  • Forms assistance (helping fill out schedules and statements)
  • Procedural questions (deadlines, filing requirements, hearing procedures)
  • General educational guidance (what each chapter does, what to expect)
  • Referrals to free legal aid for complex cases

Court help desks generally cannot give legal advice (avoid the unauthorized practice of law). They focus on procedure and general information.

Court-published resources

Most bankruptcy courts publish pro se filer packets including:

  • Required forms with instructions
  • Procedural checklists
  • Local rule summaries
  • Filing-fee waiver application (Form 103B for Chapter 7)

Quality varies substantially. Some courts publish detailed step-by-step guides; others provide only the bare forms.

Free legal-aid organizations

State and local legal-aid programs sometimes provide free representation for bankruptcy:

  • Income-eligibility requirements (usually 125-200% of federal poverty line)
  • Case-type restrictions (some only handle Chapter 7; others cover both)
  • Geographic coverage limits

The American Bar Association's "Free Legal Answers" program and law-school clinics in some areas also provide pro bono bankruptcy assistance.

Bankruptcy clinics at law schools

Several law schools operate consumer-bankruptcy clinics where supervised law students represent pro bono clients:

  • Harvard Predatory Lending and Consumer Protection Clinic
  • Stanford Consumer & Bankruptcy Clinic
  • UC Berkeley East Bay Community Law Center
  • Various law schools (Mizzou, UMKC, SLU, UIUC, Creighton, etc.) with bankruptcy or consumer-protection components

Online educational resources

For non-attorney educational content (not legal advice):

  • This site (Open Bankruptcy Project) and its research subdomains
  • The U.S. Trustee Program's debtor education resources
  • Law-school-published consumer-bankruptcy guides
  • The Federal Reserve's consumer-finance education materials

What to do if your local court has limited resources

If your district has minimal pro se assistance:

  1. Look for clinics in adjacent districts (some clinics serve multi-district areas)
  2. Check whether the state bar has a consumer-bankruptcy section with pro bono coordinators
  3. Contact local legal-aid offices for referrals or limited representation
  4. If complex case, consider unbundled legal services (paying an attorney for specific tasks rather than full representation)
